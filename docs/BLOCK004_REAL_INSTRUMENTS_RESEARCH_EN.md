# Block 4. Real Isolated Instrument Notes

## Full working description of the logic, sequence, and current conclusion

Status: `ongoing research`, but already with a very strong confirmed result at the level of isolated real notes.

---

## 1. Purpose of Block 4

Block 4 is not primarily about melody recognition and not yet about final ensemble separation.

Its job is deeper:

- take **real recordings of isolated notes** from different instruments;
- observe how each note lives in time;
- separate:
  - primary note excitation,
  - stable harmonic chain,
  - instrument body response,
  - secondary and residual resonance;
- build from this a **behavioral passport of the instrument**;
- and only then transfer that knowledge into mixed and polyphonic audio.

In other words, Block 4 answers the question:

> what exactly does a real instrument do when one of its notes sounds?

Without that layer, the later question

> who exactly is sounding inside a shared audio stream?

remains under-grounded.

---

## 2. Initial research problem

At early stages it was natural to suspect that a single-note recording contains:

- useful information,
- noise,
- and an "empty tail" without meaningful structure.

But the visual and structural work in Block 4 gradually showed a different picture:

- a substantial part of what first looks like noise
- is actually a non-random continuation of the resonance life of the note;
- in other words, not "junk", but **secondary causal structure**.

The newer lineage visualization strengthened this even more:

- we no longer see only a cloud of points around a note,
- but **which harmonic appears to generate which resonance chain**.

This changes the interpretation of the data in a fundamental way.

---

## 3. Main hypothesis of Block 4

The current working hypothesis is:

> an isolated real note is not "main sound + random noise".
> It is a causally connected resonance system:
> harmonic cores, resonance chains generated by them,
> instrument-body responses, and secondary environmental responses.

This does not mean that every weak remainder is already fully explained.

It means the default perspective changes:

- we stop searching for "empty" leftovers,
- and start searching for **unresolved causal layers**.

## 6. What the harmonic-lineage 3D stage added

The new `harmonic_chain_spiral3d` layer made a major difference.

It takes the already built spiral3d points and asks:

- which points belong to harmonic core;
- which note-box points appear to be generated by that core;
- which residual points appear to be generated by that core;
- which points still cannot yet be confidently assigned.

This produces a lineage-oriented space with roles such as:

- `harmonic_core`
- `spawned_note_box`
- `spawned_residual`
- `unassigned_resonance`

The crucial result is that the note no longer looks like:

- one central line plus meaningless dust,

but rather like:

- a set of linked resonance continuations around generating harmonic centers.

## 7. What `unassigned resonance` means in ideal conditions

This point is especially important for Block 4 because we are not dealing with a noisy ensemble scene here.
We are dealing with isolated notes recorded in highly controlled conditions.

So if `unassigned resonance` still remains even there, it does **not** mean:

- "the recording contains garbage",
- or "the tail is empty".

It more likely means:

- the note remains causally richer than the current lineage assignment rule;
- some responses do not live as a direct child of one nearest harmonic core;
- some responses are more distant, more layered, or more indirect than the current model can comfortably assign.

In Block 4, `unassigned resonance` is therefore better understood as:

- not noise,
- not empty information,
- but a **currently unresolved yet potentially meaningful causal response**.

It may even be a very distant response, figuratively a "thousandth-order" echo,
but still important for the full life of the note.

---

## 8. Working classification of `unassigned resonance`

At the current stage it is reasonable to treat `unassigned resonance` as a mixture of several subtypes:

1. `late body return`
   a delayed response of the instrument body that has already moved far enough from the nearest core to escape a simple nearest-parent rule;

2. `secondary unresolved branch`
   a secondary branch that does not go directly `harmonic core -> point`, but through an intermediate resonance step;

3. `peripheral field response`
   a field-like response still causally tied to the note, but already living at the edge of the main trajectory;

4. `very high-order resonance response`
   a weak but systematic distant response that belongs to a long causal chain.

The fourth type is especially important for the general theory:
small amplitude does not necessarily mean small significance.

Some responses may be late, weak, and hard to assign,
while still being structurally necessary for the full resonance life of the note.

---

## 9. Why the model does not yet assign all such points

The current lineage builder still behaves mainly as a local heuristic assigner:

- it searches for the nearest harmonic core;
- it uses `x12/y12` proximity;
- it uses temporal proximity;
- it prefers direct rather than multi-step causality.

So everything that lives:

- too late,
- too weak,
- too peripherally,
- or through intermediate resonance steps,

often falls into `unassigned`.

This should be interpreted not as a failure of the data,
but as a boundary of the present formalization.

---

## 10. Why this changes the general interpretation

If everything outside the core chain behaved like chaotic residue,
we could say:

- yes, the main note is structured,
- and the rest is noise.

But the observations show something else:

- the remaining mass does not collapse into noise,
- it forms resonance continuations;
- these continuations are spatially and temporally organized;
- they tend to stay around specific harmonic centers.

That means Block 4 gives us a new baseline:

> in isolated real notes, there are no strong grounds to treat the remaining content as empty noise.
> It is more accurate to treat it as causal resonance life that is only partly resolved so far.

## 11. Practical outputs

Block 4 now produces not only note-level reports, but also passport-level summaries, including:

- dense behavior across the range;
- instrument box behavior;
- note-box behavior;
- box-to-harmonic relation;
- harmonic morphology compare;
- harmonic lineage spiral3d summaries.

These outputs are intended to become reusable instrument passports for later ensemble analysis.

---

## 12. Current limitations

Despite the strong result, some limitations remain:

1. `parent_harmonic_index` assignment is still heuristic:
   - by `x12/y12` proximity,
   - and by temporal proximity.

2. `unassigned_resonance` is not yet fully decomposed into:
   - late body return,
   - secondary lineage branch,
   - peripheral field response,
   - and very high-order distant responses.

3. Full lineage visualization across all notes can still be heavy in runtime and HTML size.

4. At this stage the instrument passport is stronger as a research description of behavior
   than as a fully finished physical source-separation module.

---

## 13. Final short conclusion

Block 4 shows that an isolated real instrument note should not be understood as
"a clean tone plus meaningless noisy tail",
but as a multi-layer resonance system.

Dense scan, harmonic chain, root consensus, instrument box, note-box profile,
spiral3d, harmonic morphology, and the newer harmonic-lineage 3D layer
together support one strong conclusion:

there are no convincing grounds to treat the residual content of isolated notes as empty noise;
it is more accurate to see in it the causal life of harmonics,
the chains generated by them,
instrument-body response,
secondary environmental response,
and even still-unresolved distant responses that currently fall into `unassigned resonance`.
